Introduction: From Ratios to Functions

In the previous modules, we've explored trigonometric functions as ratios in right triangles and as coordinates on the unit circle. We've learned that sine and cosine take any angle as input and give us a number as output. But we haven't yet visualized what these functions look like when we graph them.

This visualization is crucial. When you graph $y = \sin(x)$, something remarkable emerges: a beautiful, wavelike pattern that repeats forever. This wave pattern isn't just mathematically interesting—it's fundamental to understanding nature and technology. Ocean waves, sound waves, alternating current, the light from your screen, radio signals, the vibration of guitar strings—all of these have complex wave patterns that can be understood and analyzed as combinations of sine waves.

Euler's Revolutionary Insight: Trigonometry as Functions

Leonhard Euler (1707-1783) was perhaps the most prolific mathematician in history, producing nearly 900 books and papers. But one of his most profound contributions was conceptual: he transformed trigonometry from a study of triangle measurements into a study of functions.

Before Euler, mathematicians thought of sine and cosine primarily as ratios—tools for solving triangles. Euler realized these were actually functions in their own right, with inputs (angles) and outputs (numbers). He introduced the modern notation we use today: $\sin(x)$, $\cos(x)$, $\tan(x)$. More remarkably, he showed that these functions could be expressed as infinite series—formulas that add up infinitely many terms.

Euler discovered the connection between trigonometric functions and the exponential function through the formula $e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$ (where $i$ represents the imaginary unit from complex numbers—a fundamental mathematical concept we'll explore in later lessons), which we'll investigate further in advanced modules. This insight revealed that oscillating phenomena (described by sine and cosine) and exponential growth/decay are fundamentally related—a connection that became essential in physics and engineering.

By graphing these functions and studying their properties systematically, Euler laid the groundwork for Fourier analysis (which we introduced in Module 1, and will explore in more detail in Module 7) and much of modern mathematical physics. His work showed that understanding the shape of these functions—their waves, their repetition, their transformations—was just as important as calculating specific values.

4.1 Visualizing Sine: From Circle to Wave

Let's start by understanding where the sine wave comes from. Remember that on the unit circle, for any angle $\theta$, the point $P$ has coordinates $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$. The $y$-coordinate of this point is $\sin\theta$.

Now imagine "unwrapping" the unit circle. As the angle increases from $0$ to $2\pi$ (one complete rotation), we track how the $y$-coordinate changes. This creates the sine wave.

Fig. 4.1-1: Unwrapping the Unit Circle to Create the Sine Wave
Left: A point moving around the unit circle. Right: The y-coordinate (sine value) plotted against the angle. As the point completes one rotation, we see one complete cycle of the sine wave.

Let's examine the graph of $y = \sin(x)$ more carefully:

Fig. 4.1-2: Graph of y = sin(x)
The sine function over two complete periods. Notice the smooth, wavelike pattern that repeats every 2π radians (360°).

Key Properties of the Sine Graph

  • Domain: All real numbers. You can input any angle, no matter how large or small.
  • Range: $[-1, 1]$. The sine value is always between -1 and 1 (inclusive).
  • Period: $2\pi$ (or 360°). The pattern repeats every $2\pi$ radians.
  • Amplitude: 1. The wave oscillates 1 unit above and below the x-axis.
  • Zeros: $x = n\pi$ where $n$ is any integer (0, ±π, ±2π, ...).
  • Maximum values: 1, occurring at $x = \frac{\pi}{2} + 2n\pi$ (90°, 450°, ...).
  • Minimum values: -1, occurring at $x = \frac{3\pi}{2} + 2n\pi$ (270°, 630°, ...).
Why does the period equal 2π? Because that's one complete rotation around the unit circle. After rotating $2\pi$ radians (360°), you return to your starting position, and the sine values begin repeating. This connection to circular motion is why radians are so natural for trigonometric functions.
Problem 1: Looking at the graph of $y = \sin(x)$, estimate the values of:
(a) $\sin(\pi/6)$
(b) $\sin(\pi)$
(c) $\sin(3\pi/2)$
(d) $\sin(5\pi)$
Solution:
(a) $\sin(\pi/6) = 0.5$ (this is 30°, halfway between 0 and the maximum)
(b) $\sin(\pi) = 0$ (180°, crosses the x-axis)
(c) $\sin(3\pi/2) = -1$ (270°, at the minimum)
(d) $\sin(5\pi) = 0$ (two and a half periods; $5\pi = 2\pi + 2\pi + \pi$, and $\sin(\pi) = 0$)

4.2 Visualizing Cosine

The cosine function has a graph very similar to sine, but shifted. Recall that on the unit circle, $\cos\theta$ is the $x$-coordinate of the point at angle $\theta$.

Fig. 4.1-3: Graph of y = cos(x)
The cosine function over two complete periods. Compare this to the sine graph—they have the same shape but are shifted horizontally.

Key Properties of the Cosine Graph

  • Domain: All real numbers.
  • Range: $[-1, 1]$.
  • Period: $2\pi$.
  • Amplitude: 1.
  • Zeros: $x = \frac{\pi}{2} + n\pi$ (90°, 270°, 450°, ...).
  • Maximum values: 1, occurring at $x = 2n\pi$ (0°, 360°, 720°, ...).
  • Minimum values: -1, occurring at $x = \pi + 2n\pi$ (180°, 540°, ...).

The Relationship Between Sine and Cosine

Let's compare the two graphs directly:

Fig. 4.1-4: Comparing Sine and Cosine
Sine (blue) and cosine (red) graphed together. Notice that cosine is just sine shifted π/2 radians (90°) to the left.

The key observation: $\cos(x) = \sin(x + \pi/2)$. This identity reflects what we learned earlier: cosine of an angle equals sine of the complementary angle. Graphically, this means the cosine curve is the sine curve shifted $\pi/2$ units to the left.

Problem 2: Without using a calculator, determine which is larger:
(a) $\sin(1)$ or $\cos(1)$ (angles in radians)
(b) $\sin(2)$ or $\cos(2)$
(Hint: Use the graphs to visualize the answer.)
Solution:
(a) Since 1 radian ≈ 57°, we're in the first quadrant where both are positive. Looking at the graphs, at $x = 1$, the sine curve is higher than the cosine curve, so $\sin(1) > \cos(1)$.
(b) 2 radians ≈ 115°, still in the second quadrant. At $x = 2$, sine is still positive but cosine has become negative, so $\sin(2) > \cos(2)$.

4.3 Visualizing Tangent

The tangent function, $\tan(x) = \frac{\sin(x)}{\cos(x)}$, has a very different graph from sine and cosine. Because tangent is a ratio involving cosine in the denominator, something dramatic happens when $\cos(x) = 0$.

Fig. 4.1-5: Graph of y = tan(x)
The tangent function showing three periods. The red dashed lines mark the vertical asymptotes where the function is undefined (at x = ±π/2, where cosine equals zero).

Key Properties of the Tangent Graph

  • Domain: All real numbers except $x = \frac{\pi}{2} + n\pi$ (where $\cos(x) = 0$).
  • Range: All real numbers. Unlike sine and cosine, tangent can take any value.
  • Period: $\pi$ (not $2\pi$!). The tangent pattern repeats every $\pi$ radians (180°).
  • No amplitude: Tangent doesn't oscillate between fixed bounds; it increases without limit.
  • Zeros: $x = n\pi$ (0°, ±180°, ±360°, ...), same as sine.
  • Vertical asymptotes: At $x = \frac{\pi}{2} + n\pi$ (±90°, ±270°, ...).
Understanding the asymptotes: As $x$ approaches $\frac{\pi}{2}$ (90°), the cosine approaches 0, which means we're dividing by a number closer and closer to zero. This causes the tangent value to increase without limit—it approaches infinity. This concept of approaching a value is fundamental in calculus (called a "limit"), and we see it visually here as the vertical asymptotes.

Why Is the Period π Instead of 2π?

Notice that $\tan(x)$ has the same period as half a circle rotation, not a full rotation. This is because:

$\tan(x + \pi) = \frac{\sin(x + \pi)}{\cos(x + \pi)} = \frac{-\sin(x)}{-\cos(x)} = \frac{\sin(x)}{\cos(x)} = \tan(x)$

Both sine and cosine flip sign when you add $\pi$, so the ratio stays the same. Geometrically, opposite points on the unit circle (differing by 180°) have the same slope—which is what tangent represents.

Problem 3:
(a) What happens to $\tan(x)$ as $x$ approaches $\frac{\pi}{2}$ from the left?
(b) What happens as $x$ approaches $\frac{\pi}{2}$ from the right?
(c) Why is $\tan(x)$ undefined at exactly $x = \frac{\pi}{2}$?
Solution:
(a) As $x$ approaches $\frac{\pi}{2}$ from the left, $\tan(x)$ increases without bound (approaches $+\infty$).
(b) As $x$ approaches $\frac{\pi}{2}$ from the right, $\tan(x)$ decreases without bound (approaches $-\infty$).
(c) At $x = \frac{\pi}{2}$, $\cos(\frac{\pi}{2}) = 0$, so we would be dividing by zero, which is undefined.

4.4 Periodic Functions: The Mathematics of Repetition

Sine, cosine, and tangent are all examples of periodic functions—functions whose values repeat at regular intervals. This concept of periodicity is fundamental in mathematics and science because so many natural phenomena repeat: day and night, seasons, ocean tides, heartbeats, planetary orbits, electromagnetic waves.

Formal Definition

A function $f(x)$ is periodic with period $p$ if:
$f(x + p) = f(x)$ for all values of $x$
The smallest positive value of $p$ for which this is true is called the fundamental period.

For sine and cosine, the fundamental period is $2\pi$. For tangent, it's $\pi$.

Frequency: The Complement of Period

The frequency of a periodic function is how many complete cycles occur in a unit interval. It's the reciprocal of the period:

$\text{frequency} = \frac{1}{\text{period}}$

For example, if a wave has period $2\pi$, its frequency is $\frac{1}{2\pi}$ cycles per unit. In physics and engineering, frequency is often measured in Hertz (Hz), which means cycles per second.

Example: Musical Notes and Frequency

The note "middle C" on a piano has a frequency of 261.6 Hz, meaning the sound wave completes 261.6 cycles per second. The period of one cycle is:

$\text{period} = \frac{1}{261.6} \approx 0.00382 \text{ seconds} \approx 3.82 \text{ milliseconds}$

The note "A above middle C" (the tuning note) has a frequency of 440 Hz, so its period is $\frac{1}{440} \approx 2.27$ milliseconds. Higher frequencies mean shorter periods—the wave oscillates faster.

Problem 4:
(a) If a function has a period of 5, what is its frequency?
(b) If a wave has a frequency of 0.2 cycles per second, what is its period?
(c) How many complete cycles of $y = \sin(x)$ occur in the interval $[0, 6\pi]$?
Solution:
(a) Frequency = $\frac{1}{5} = 0.2$ cycles per unit
(b) Period = $\frac{1}{0.2} = 5$ seconds
(c) The period of sine is $2\pi$. In the interval $[0, 6\pi]$, there are $\frac{6\pi}{2\pi} = 3$ complete cycles.

A Note on Real-World Simplification

In the sections that follow, we'll model physical phenomena like sound waves, tides, and temperature cycles as if they were simple, single-frequency sine waves. This is a pedagogical simplification to help you understand the fundamental principles of periodic functions and their transformations.

In reality, most waves in nature are not pure sine waves—they're complex patterns that result from combining many sine waves of different amplitudes, frequencies, and phases. A single musical note from a violin, for instance, contains the fundamental frequency plus many harmonics (multiples of that frequency), which is why it sounds different from a pure electronic tone at the same pitch.

Understanding how to decompose complex waves into sums of simple sine waves is the subject of Fourier analysis, which we introduced in Module 1 and will explore further in Module 7. For now, we're focusing on the building blocks—the individual sine and cosine waves—and how to transform them. Once you understand these basics, you'll be prepared to see how they combine to model the rich complexity of real-world phenomena.

4.5 Amplitude and Vertical Shift: Changing the Height

Now we'll explore how to transform trigonometric functions. We'll start with changes that affect the vertical dimension: amplitude and vertical shift.

Amplitude: Scaling the Wave

The amplitude of a wave is the distance from the center line to a peak (or to a trough). For the basic sine function $y = \sin(x)$, the amplitude is 1. But what happens when we multiply by a constant?

Consider the function $y = A\sin(x)$ where $A$ is a positive constant:

Fig. 4.2-1: Effect of Amplitude: y = A sin(x)
Comparing y = sin(x) (blue), y = 2sin(x) (red), and y = 0.5sin(x) (green). The parameter A stretches or compresses the wave vertically.
For $y = A\sin(x)$ or $y = A\cos(x)$:
  • The amplitude is $|A|$ (the absolute value of $A$)
  • The wave oscillates between $-|A|$ and $+|A|$
  • If $A$ is negative, the wave is reflected across the x-axis
  • The period remains unchanged at $2\pi$
Example: Sound Volume

Sound waves are pressure oscillations that can be modeled by sine functions. The amplitude determines the volume (loudness). A whisper might have amplitude 0.01, while a rock concert might have amplitude 100. The amplitude doesn't change the pitch (frequency) of the sound, only how loud it is.

Vertical Shift: Moving the Center Line

Adding a constant to a function shifts it vertically. For $y = \sin(x) + D$:

Fig. 4.2-2: Effect of Vertical Shift: y = sin(x) + D
Comparing y = sin(x) (blue), y = sin(x) + 2 (red), and y = sin(x) - 1.5 (green). Adding D shifts the entire wave up or down.
For $y = \sin(x) + D$ or $y = \cos(x) + D$:
  • The entire graph shifts vertically by $D$ units
  • If $D$ is positive, the wave shifts up; if $D$ is negative, the wave shifts down
  • The midline (center line) is at $y = D$
  • The wave oscillates between $D - 1$ and $D + 1$
  • The amplitude and period remain unchanged
Example: Ocean Tides

Tide height can be modeled as a sine function with a vertical shift. If the tide varies 3 feet above and below a mean sea level of 10 feet, the model would be:

$h(t) = 3\sin(\omega t) + 10$

where $h$ is height in feet and $t$ is time. The vertical shift of 10 feet represents mean sea level, and the amplitude of 3 feet represents the tidal range.

Combining Amplitude and Vertical Shift

When we combine both transformations, we get $y = A\sin(x) + D$:

Fig. 4.2-3: Combined: y = A sin(x) + D
The function y = 2sin(x) + 1 (red) compared to y = sin(x) (blue). The amplitude is 2 and the midline is y = 1.
For $y = A\sin(x) + D$:
  • Amplitude: $|A|$
  • Midline: $y = D$
  • Maximum value: $D + |A|$
  • Minimum value: $D - |A|$
  • Period: $2\pi$
Problem 5: For the function $y = 5\sin(x) - 3$:
(a) What is the amplitude?
(b) What is the vertical shift?
(c) What is the midline?
(d) What are the maximum and minimum values?
(e) Sketch a rough graph showing one complete period.
Solution:
(a) Amplitude = 5
(b) Vertical shift = -3 (shifted down 3 units)
(c) Midline is $y = -3$
(d) Maximum = $-3 + 5 = 2$; Minimum = $-3 - 5 = -8$
(e) The graph oscillates between -8 and 2, centered at y = -3, completing one cycle over $[0, 2\pi]$
Problem 6: A Ferris wheel has a diameter of 50 meters, with its center 30 meters above the ground. Your height above ground as a function of angle $\theta$ (measured from the 3 o'clock position) can be modeled as:
$h(\theta) = A\sin(\theta) + D$
Find the values of $A$ and $D$.
Solution:
The Ferris wheel radius is 25 meters (diameter ÷ 2), so the amplitude is $A = 25$.
The center is at 30 meters, so the vertical shift is $D = 30$.
Therefore: $h(\theta) = 25\sin(\theta) + 30$
This gives a maximum height of 55 meters (top of wheel) and minimum of 5 meters (bottom of wheel).

4.6 Period and Frequency: Changing the Speed

Now let's explore transformations that affect the horizontal dimension—how fast the wave oscillates.

The Parameter B: Horizontal Compression and Stretch

Consider the function $y = \sin(Bx)$ where $B$ is a positive constant. The parameter $B$ affects how quickly the function completes a cycle:

Fig. 4.3-1: Effect of B: y = sin(Bx)
Comparing y = sin(x) (blue), y = sin(2x) (red), and y = sin(0.5x) (green). The parameter B changes how fast the wave oscillates.
For $y = \sin(Bx)$ or $y = \cos(Bx)$ where $B > 0$:
  • The period is $\frac{2\pi}{B}$
  • The frequency is $\frac{B}{2\pi}$
  • Larger $B$ means shorter period (faster oscillation)
  • Smaller $B$ means longer period (slower oscillation)

Why Does the Period Equal 2π/B?

Think about what happens when we complete one cycle. For $y = \sin(x)$, one cycle occurs as $x$ goes from $0$ to $2\pi$. For $y = \sin(Bx)$, one cycle occurs when the argument $Bx$ goes from $0$ to $2\pi$:

$Bx = 2\pi$
$x = \frac{2\pi}{B}$

So the period—the distance in $x$ needed to complete one cycle—is $\frac{2\pi}{B}$.

Example: Musical Notes

The note "middle C" (261.6 Hz) completes 261.6 cycles per second. If we model the sound wave as $y = \sin(Bx)$ where $x$ is time in seconds, we need:

frequency = $\frac{B}{2\pi} = 261.6$
$B = 261.6 \times 2\pi \approx 1643$ radians per second

The period is $\frac{2\pi}{B} = \frac{1}{261.6} \approx 0.00382$ seconds, which matches our earlier calculation.

The note "A above middle C" (440 Hz) would use $B = 440 \times 2\pi \approx 2765$ radians per second—a larger $B$ for a higher frequency.

Angular Frequency in Physics

In physics and engineering, the parameter $B$ in $y = \sin(Bx)$ or $y = \cos(Bx)$ is called the angular frequency, often denoted by $\omega$ (lowercase Greek omega). It represents how many radians per unit time the oscillation sweeps through.

This notation comes from studying rotating systems. Euler's work on rotational motion and oscillations established the connection between circular motion (measured in radians) and periodic oscillations described by sine and cosine. When a point moves around a circle at constant speed, its angular position changes at a rate $\omega$ radians per second, and its projection onto an axis traces out a sine or cosine wave with angular frequency $\omega$.

Problem 7: For the function $y = \sin(4x)$:
(a) What is the period?
(b) What is the frequency?
(c) How many complete cycles occur in the interval $[0, 2\pi]$?
Solution:
(a) Period = $\frac{2\pi}{4} = \frac{\pi}{2}$
(b) Frequency = $\frac{1}{\text{period}} = \frac{1}{\pi/2} = \frac{2}{\pi}$ cycles per unit
(c) In the interval $[0, 2\pi]$, which has length $2\pi$, the number of cycles is $\frac{2\pi}{\pi/2} = 4$ cycles
Problem 8: You want to model a function that completes 5 complete cycles in the interval $[0, 10]$. What value of $B$ should you use in $y = \sin(Bx)$?
Solution:
5 cycles in a length of 10 means each cycle has length $\frac{10}{5} = 2$.
The period is 2, so: $\frac{2\pi}{B} = 2$
Solving for B: $B = \frac{2\pi}{2} = \pi$
The function is $y = \sin(\pi x)$

4.7 Phase Shift: Moving the Wave Horizontally

The final transformation we'll study is the phase shift—a horizontal shift of the wave. This is represented by $y = \sin(x - C)$ or more generally $y = \sin(B(x - C))$.

Understanding Phase Shift

Fig. 4.4-1: Effect of Phase Shift: y = sin(x - C)
Comparing y = sin(x) (blue), y = sin(x - π/2) (red), and y = sin(x + π/3) (green). The parameter C shifts the wave horizontally.
For $y = \sin(x - C)$:
  • The graph shifts right by $C$ units if $C > 0$
  • The graph shifts left by $|C|$ units if $C < 0$
  • Think: $y = \sin(x - C)$ shifts right, $y = \sin(x + C)$ shifts left

This might seem backwards at first! Why does subtracting C shift the graph to the right? Think of it this way:

For $y = \sin(x - \frac{\pi}{2})$, when does the sine equal zero? We need:

$x - \frac{\pi}{2} = 0$
$x = \frac{\pi}{2}$

So the zero that was at $x = 0$ for $y = \sin(x)$ is now at $x = \frac{\pi}{2}$—shifted right by $\frac{\pi}{2}$.

Why Is It Called "Phase"?

The term "phase" comes from astronomy and physics. When two waves have the same frequency but different starting positions, they're said to have a phase difference. This concept is crucial for understanding interference of waves.

Example: Noise-Canceling Headphones

Noise-canceling headphones work by detecting external sound waves and producing a wave that's phase-shifted by exactly $\pi$ radians (180°). If the external noise is $y = \sin(x)$, the headphones produce $y = \sin(x - \pi) = -\sin(x)$. When these waves combine, they cancel out:

$\sin(x) + \sin(x - \pi) = \sin(x) - \sin(x) = 0$

This is called destructive interference.

Fig. 4.4-2: Destructive Interference: How Noise Cancellation Works
Blue: external noise sin(x). Red: headphone signal sin(x - π) = -sin(x). Green: their sum equals zero everywhere—complete cancellation.

Phase Shift with Period Change

When we combine period change and phase shift, we get $y = \sin(B(x - C))$. The phase shift is $C$ units, but be careful with the order of operations:

For $y = \sin(B(x - C))$ or $y = \cos(B(x - C))$:
  • The period is $\frac{2\pi}{B}$
  • The phase shift is $C$ units to the right (if $C > 0$)
  • Note: The phase shift is $C$, not $BC$

Important: If you see $y = \sin(Bx - C)$, you must first factor out $B$ to find the actual phase shift:

$y = \sin(Bx - C) = \sin(B(x - \frac{C}{B}))$

So the phase shift is $\frac{C}{B}$, not $C$.

Problem 9: For $y = \sin(2(x - \frac{\pi}{4}))$:
(a) What is the period?
(b) What is the phase shift?
(c) Where does the first maximum occur (for $x > 0$)?
Solution:
(a) Period = $\frac{2\pi}{2} = \pi$
(b) Phase shift = $\frac{\pi}{4}$ to the right
(c) For $y = \sin(x)$, the first maximum is at $x = \frac{\pi}{2}$. With our transformations, this occurs when $2(x - \frac{\pi}{4}) = \frac{\pi}{2}$:
$x - \frac{\pi}{4} = \frac{\pi}{4}$
$x = \frac{\pi}{2}$
Problem 10: Convert $y = \cos(3x - \pi)$ into the form $y = \cos(B(x - C))$ and identify the period and phase shift.
Solution:
Factor out the 3: $y = \cos(3x - \pi) = \cos(3(x - \frac{\pi}{3}))$
Period = $\frac{2\pi}{3}$
Phase shift = $\frac{\pi}{3}$ to the right

4.8 The General Form: Putting It All Together

We can now combine all four transformations into the most general form of a sinusoidal function:

$y = A\sin(B(x - C)) + D$
or
$y = A\cos(B(x - C)) + D$

Let's understand what each parameter controls:

Parameters of the General Sinusoidal Function
For $y = A\sin(B(x - C)) + D$:
  • $|A|$ = amplitude (height of oscillation from midline)
  • $\frac{2\pi}{B}$ = period (length of one complete cycle)
  • $C$ = phase shift (horizontal shift; right if positive)
  • $D$ = vertical shift (midline location)

Derived properties:
  • Maximum value = $D + |A|$
  • Minimum value = $D - |A|$
  • Midline: $y = D$
  • Frequency = $\frac{B}{2\pi}$ cycles per unit
Fig. 4.5-1: General Form: y = A sin(B(x - C)) + D
The function y = 3sin(2(x - π/4)) + 1 (red) compared to y = sin(x) (blue). Amplitude = 3, period = π, phase shift = π/4 right, vertical shift = 1.

Analyzing a Function: A Systematic Approach

Example: Complete Analysis

Analyze the function $y = -2\cos(3(x + \frac{\pi}{6})) - 1$

Step 1: Rewrite in standard form: $y = -2\cos(3(x - (-\frac{\pi}{6}))) + (-1)$

Step 2: Identify parameters:
• $A = -2$ (amplitude is $|A| = 2$; negative means reflected)
• $B = 3$
• $C = -\frac{\pi}{6}$ (shifts left, since negative)
• $D = -1$

Step 3: Calculate properties:
• Amplitude = 2
• Period = $\frac{2\pi}{3}$
• Phase shift = $\frac{\pi}{6}$ to the left
• Midline: $y = -1$
• Maximum value = $-1 + 2 = 1$ (but reached at minima of cosine due to reflection)
• Minimum value = $-1 - 2 = -3$ (reached at maxima of cosine due to reflection)

Writing an Equation from a Graph

Often you'll need to work backwards—given a graph, find the equation. Here's a systematic approach:

Strategy for Finding the Equation
  1. Find D: The midline is $y = D$ (average of max and min values)
  2. Find A: Amplitude $= \frac{\text{max} - \text{min}}{2}$
  3. Find B: Measure the period $p$, then $B = \frac{2\pi}{p}$
  4. Find C: Identify where a key feature occurs compared to the parent function
  5. Choose sine or cosine: Use cosine if the graph starts at a maximum or minimum; use sine if it starts at the midline
Problem 11: A sinusoidal function has:
• Maximum value of 7 at $x = 2$
• Minimum value of -1
• Period of 8
Write an equation for this function using cosine.
Solution:
Step 1: Find D (midline): $D = \frac{7 + (-1)}{2} = 3$
Step 2: Find A (amplitude): $A = \frac{7 - (-1)}{2} = 4$
Step 3: Find B: Period = 8, so $\frac{2\pi}{B} = 8$, giving $B = \frac{2\pi}{8} = \frac{\pi}{4}$
Step 4: Find C: For standard cosine, the first maximum is at $x = 0$. Our maximum is at $x = 2$, so we need a phase shift of 2 to the right: $C = 2$
Answer: $y = 4\cos(\frac{\pi}{4}(x - 2)) + 3$

Verification: At $x = 2$: $y = 4\cos(0) + 3 = 4(1) + 3 = 7$ ✓
Problem 12: For the function $y = 5\sin(4x - \pi) + 2$:
(a) Rewrite in the form $y = A\sin(B(x - C)) + D$
(b) State the amplitude, period, phase shift, and midline
(c) What are the maximum and minimum values?
Solution:
(a) Factor out 4: $y = 5\sin(4(x - \frac{\pi}{4})) + 2$

(b) Amplitude = 5
Period = $\frac{2\pi}{4} = \frac{\pi}{2}$
Phase shift = $\frac{\pi}{4}$ to the right
Midline: $y = 2$

(c) Maximum = $2 + 5 = 7$
Minimum = $2 - 5 = -3$

4.9 Applications of Periodic Functions

Now that we understand how to transform trigonometric functions, let's see how these transformations model real-world periodic phenomena. Remember from our earlier discussion that we'll be modeling these phenomena as single-frequency sine waves for simplicity. In reality, phenomena like tides and temperature involve multiple frequencies combined together, but understanding the single-wave model is the essential first step. (Note that some applications, like AC electrical current and simple harmonic motion in springs, are genuinely well-approximated by single sine waves, while others like tides and weather are more complex.)

Application 1: Ocean Tides

Example: Modeling Tide Heights

At a certain harbor, the water depth varies sinusoidally. High tide occurs at 6:00 AM with a depth of 15 feet. Low tide occurs 6 hours later with a depth of 5 feet. Model the depth $d$ (in feet) as a function of time $t$ (in hours after midnight).

Solution:

Step 1: Find the parameters:
• Midline: $D = \frac{15 + 5}{2} = 10$ feet
• Amplitude: $A = \frac{15 - 5}{2} = 5$ feet
• Period: High tide to low tide is half a period, so period = 12 hours
• Therefore: $B = \frac{2\pi}{12} = \frac{\pi}{6}$

Step 2: Choose the function and phase shift:
Since high tide (maximum) occurs at $t = 6$, we'll use cosine with a phase shift:
$d(t) = 5\cos(\frac{\pi}{6}(t - 6)) + 10$

Step 3: Verify:
• At $t = 6$: $d(6) = 5\cos(0) + 10 = 15$ ✓ (high tide)
• At $t = 12$: $d(12) = 5\cos(\pi) + 10 = -5 + 10 = 5$ ✓ (low tide)

Fig. 4.6-1: Tide Model: d(t) = 5cos(π(t-6)/6) + 10
Water depth over 24 hours. High tides occur at 6 AM and 6 PM (depth = 15 ft). Low tides occur at noon and midnight (depth = 5 ft).

Application 2: Temperature Cycles

Example: Daily Temperature Variation

In a desert, the temperature varies sinusoidally throughout the day. The average temperature is 75°F. The maximum temperature of 95°F occurs at 3:00 PM (15 hours after midnight). Find a function modeling temperature $T$ as a function of time $t$ (hours after midnight).

Solution:
• Midline: $D = 75°$ (given as average)
• Amplitude: Maximum is 95°, so $A = 95 - 75 = 20°$
• Period: 24 hours, so $B = \frac{2\pi}{24} = \frac{\pi}{12}$
• Phase shift: Maximum at $t = 15$, so $C = 15$

Model: $T(t) = 20\cos(\frac{\pi}{12}(t - 15)) + 75$

This tells us the minimum temperature is $75 - 20 = 55°$F, occurring at 3:00 AM.

Application 3: Alternating Current

Example: AC Voltage

In North America, household electrical outlets provide alternating current (AC) at 60 Hz with a peak voltage of approximately 170 volts. The voltage oscillates sinusoidally. Model the voltage $V$ as a function of time $t$ (in seconds).

Solution:
• Amplitude: $A = 170$ volts
• Frequency: 60 Hz means 60 cycles per second
• Period: $\frac{1}{60}$ seconds
• Therefore: $B = \frac{2\pi}{\text{period}} = 2\pi \cdot 60 = 120\pi$ radians per second

Model: $V(t) = 170\sin(120\pi t)$

Note: We use sine starting at zero, since voltage is zero at $t = 0$ (the conventional reference point). The voltage oscillates 60 times per second between +170V and -170V.

RMS Voltage: The "120 volts" stated for outlets is actually the RMS (root mean square) value: $\frac{170}{\sqrt{2}} \approx 120$ volts, which represents the effective voltage for power calculations.

Application 4: Simple Harmonic Motion (Springs and Pendulums)

Example: Mass on a Spring

A mass attached to a spring is pulled down 10 cm from its equilibrium position and released. It oscillates up and down, completing one full oscillation every 2 seconds. Model the displacement $y$ (in cm, positive upward) as a function of time $t$ (in seconds).

Solution:
• Amplitude: $A = 10$ cm (starts at maximum displacement)
• Period: 2 seconds, so $B = \frac{2\pi}{2} = \pi$
• Starts at minimum ($y = -10$ at $t = 0$), so we use negative cosine:

Model: $y(t) = -10\cos(\pi t)$ or equivalently $y(t) = 10\cos(\pi t - \pi)$

At $t = 0$: $y = -10$ cm (maximum downward displacement)
At $t = 0.5$: $y = 0$ (passing through equilibrium)
At $t = 1$: $y = 10$ cm (maximum upward displacement)
At $t = 1.5$: $y = 0$ (passing through equilibrium again)
At $t = 2$: $y = -10$ cm (back to starting position)

Problem 13: The London Eye is a giant Ferris wheel with a diameter of 135 meters. Its center is 67.5 meters above the Thames River. It completes one rotation every 30 minutes. A passenger boards at the bottom of the wheel at time $t = 0$. Write a function $h(t)$ giving the passenger's height above the river as a function of time (in minutes).
Solution:
• Amplitude: $A = \frac{135}{2} = 67.5$ meters (radius of wheel)
• Midline: $D = 67.5$ meters (height of center)
• Period: 30 minutes, so $B = \frac{2\pi}{30} = \frac{\pi}{15}$
• Starting at bottom (minimum), so use negative cosine:

$h(t) = -67.5\cos(\frac{\pi}{15}t) + 67.5 = 67.5(1 - \cos(\frac{\pi}{15}t))$

This gives: minimum height = 0 meters (at $t = 0$), maximum height = 135 meters (at $t = 15$ minutes).

4.10 Wave Interference and Superposition

One of the most important properties of waves is that they can be combined. When two waves occupy the same space, they add together—a principle called superposition. This leads to fascinating phenomena that are fundamental to understanding sound, light, and many other wave behaviors.

Adding Sine Waves

When we add two sinusoidal functions, we get a new function. The result depends on the amplitudes, frequencies, and phase relationships of the original waves.

Fig. 4.6-2: Adding Two Waves with Same Frequency
Blue: y = sin(x); Red: y = sin(x + π/4); Green: y = sin(x) + sin(x + π/4). The sum is still sinusoidal with the same frequency.

When two waves have the same frequency but different amplitudes or phases, their sum is another sinusoidal wave with the same frequency. The amplitude and phase of the result depend on how the original waves are aligned.

Constructive and Destructive Interference

Constructive Interference: When two waves are "in phase" (peaks align with peaks), they reinforce each other. The amplitude of the sum is the sum of the individual amplitudes.

Destructive Interference: When two waves are "out of phase" by $\pi$ radians (peaks align with troughs), they cancel each other. If they have equal amplitude, the result is zero.
Example: Complete Cancellation

Consider: $y_1 = \sin(x)$ and $y_2 = \sin(x + \pi)$

Since $\sin(x + \pi) = -\sin(x)$, we have:

$y_1 + y_2 = \sin(x) + (-\sin(x)) = 0$

The waves completely cancel—this is how noise-canceling headphones work!

Beats: Adding Waves with Different Frequencies

When we add two waves with slightly different frequencies, something interesting happens—we get a phenomenon called beats.

Fig. 4.6-3: Beats: Adding Waves with Close Frequencies
Blue: y = sin(10x); Red: y = sin(11x); Green: y = sin(10x) + sin(11x). The sum creates a "beating" pattern—periodic variations in amplitude.

The beat frequency is the difference between the two original frequencies. Musicians use this phenomenon when tuning instruments—when two notes are slightly out of tune, you hear a "wah-wah-wah" beating sound. As you adjust the tuning closer, the beats slow down, and when the notes match exactly, the beating stops.

Example: Musical Tuning

Two guitar strings are meant to play the same note (440 Hz), but one is slightly out of tune at 443 Hz. When played together, you'll hear beats at a frequency of:

$\text{beat frequency} = |443 - 440| = 3 \text{ Hz}$

This means you'll hear 3 "wah" pulses per second—slow enough to be distinctly audible and annoying, prompting you to tune the string.

Preview: Fourier Series

Building Complex Waves from Simple Sines

In Module 1, we introduced Fourier's revolutionary insight: any periodic function can be represented as a sum of sine and cosine waves. Now that you understand how to add waves together, you can glimpse how this works.

Consider building a square wave (which alternates between +1 and -1) using sine functions:

Fig. 4.7-1: Approximating a Square Wave with Sine Terms
Step 1: Just the fundamental: y = sin(x)
Step 2: Adding the first harmonic: y = sin(x) + sin(3x)/3. Notice the waveform starting to flatten at the peaks.
Step 3: Adding more terms: y = sin(x) + sin(3x)/3 + sin(5x)/5. The approximation to a square wave improves. A full Fourier series would continue adding terms systematically—the method provides a formula for finding each successive term to approximate any periodic function.

The Fourier series for a square wave is:

$f(x) = \frac{4}{\pi}\left(\sin(x) + \frac{\sin(3x)}{3} + \frac{\sin(5x)}{5} + \frac{\sin(7x)}{7} + ...\right)$

As we add more terms, the sum gets closer and closer to a perfect square wave. This is the essence of Fourier analysis—decomposing complex signals into simple sinusoidal components. We'll explore this more deeply in Module 7.

Applications of Wave Superposition

Signal Processing and Audio

When you listen to music, every sound you hear is a complex combination of many sine waves at different frequencies and amplitudes. Your ear naturally performs a Fourier analysis, separating these frequencies so your brain can perceive pitch, timbre, and harmony.

Modern audio technology exploits this:

  • MP3 compression: Analyzes audio into frequency components, then discards frequencies humans can't hear well
  • Equalizers: Boost or reduce specific frequency ranges (bass, midrange, treble)
  • Synthesizers: Build complex sounds by combining simple sine waves (called "additive synthesis")

Gaming and Graphics: Combining Rotations

In video games and 3D graphics, objects often undergo multiple simultaneous rotations. Each rotation can be represented using sine and cosine functions, and the combined effect is the sum of these individual motions.

Example: Planetary Motion in a Game

A moon orbits a planet, which orbits a star. If the star is at the origin, the moon's position involves adding two circular motions:

$x(t) = R\cos(\omega_1 t) + r\cos(\omega_2 t)$
$y(t) = R\sin(\omega_1 t) + r\sin(\omega_2 t)$

where $R$ and $\omega_1$ describe the planet's orbit around the star, and $r$ and $\omega_2$ describe the moon's orbit around the planet. The resulting path is a complex curve called an epicycloid.

Problem 14: Two sound waves are given by:
$y_1 = 2\sin(400\pi t)$ and $y_2 = 2\sin(404\pi t)$
where $t$ is time in seconds.
(a) What are the frequencies of these two waves (in Hz)?
(b) When these waves are played together, what beat frequency will you hear?
(c) How many beats per second will you perceive?
Solution:
(a) For $y_1$: $B = 400\pi$, so frequency = $\frac{B}{2\pi} = \frac{400\pi}{2\pi} = 200$ Hz
For $y_2$: $B = 404\pi$, so frequency = $\frac{404\pi}{2\pi} = 202$ Hz

(b) Beat frequency = $|202 - 200| = 2$ Hz

(c) You'll hear 2 beats per second (a "wah-wah" pulse twice per second)
Problem 15: In a video game, a satellite orbits Earth at 2 revolutions per minute, while simultaneously spinning around its own axis at 10 revolutions per minute. If we track a point on the satellite's surface that's 1 unit from its center, and the satellite's orbit radius is 5 units, write functions for the $x$ and $y$ coordinates of this point as a function of time $t$ (in minutes). Assume at $t=0$ everything starts at maximum $x$ position.
Solution:
For the orbital motion: 2 rev/min means $\omega_1 = 2 \times 2\pi = 4\pi$ rad/min
For the spin: 10 rev/min means $\omega_2 = 10 \times 2\pi = 20\pi$ rad/min

$x(t) = 5\cos(4\pi t) + 1\cos(20\pi t)$
$y(t) = 5\sin(4\pi t) + 1\sin(20\pi t)$

The first term represents the satellite's orbit around Earth, the second represents the point's motion around the satellite's center.

From Euler to Modern Applications

Euler's insight—that trigonometric functions should be studied as functions rather than just triangle ratios—opened the door to modern signal processing, wave mechanics, and Fourier analysis. His introduction of the exponential-trigonometric connection through $e^{ix} = \cos x + i\sin x$ revealed that oscillation and exponential growth/decay are two faces of the same mathematical coin.

Today, every time you stream music, use GPS, or play a video game with realistic physics, you're benefiting from the mathematics of periodic functions that Euler helped systematize in the 18th century. The waves we've studied in this module are the same waves that carry information across the internet, that create the images on your screen, and that describe the quantum behavior of electrons in atoms.

In Module 7, we'll return to Fourier's revolutionary insight and see how the infinite series representations that Euler discovered (called Taylor series) provide the theoretical foundation for representing any periodic function as a sum of sines and cosines. These series, which we'll discuss further in a future calculus lesson, were essential to Fourier's proof that his decomposition method works.
