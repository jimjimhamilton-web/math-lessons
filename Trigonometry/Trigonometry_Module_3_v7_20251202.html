3.1 Beyond Right Triangles: The Need for Extension

In Module 2, we defined the trigonometric functions using right triangles. This gave us a powerful tool for solving problems involving angles between 0° and 90°—measuring heights, distances, and working with various practical applications. But there's a fundamental limitation to this approach.

Consider these questions:

  • What is sin(120°)? In a right triangle, we can't even have an angle of 120° because the right angle already uses up 90°.
  • How do we describe a wheel that has rotated 450°? That's more than a full rotation!
  • What about negative angles? What does sin(-30°) mean?
  • In physics and engineering, we often need to describe periodic motion—things that repeat. How can a definition based on fixed triangles capture that?

These aren't just theoretical curiosities. In the real world, angles routinely go beyond 0° to 90°:

Navigation: A ship's bearing might be 135° (southeast), 225° (southwest), or 315° (northwest).

Rotational Motion: A spinning wheel, a rotating gear, or an orbiting planet can turn through any angle—including multiple complete rotations.

Oscillations: A pendulum swings back and forth, a spring bounces up and down, an AC current alternates—all periodic motions that need angles beyond 90°.

Computer Graphics: Rotating a sprite in a game, calculating camera angles, or animating objects requires angles in all directions.

We need a way to extend our trigonometric functions to all angles—positive, negative, greater than 360°—while remaining consistent with our right triangle definitions for angles between 0° and 90°. The solution is elegant and powerful: the unit circle.

3.2 The Unit Circle: A Foundation for All Angles

What Is the Unit Circle?

The unit circle is simply a circle with radius 1, centered at the origin of a coordinate system. That's it. But this simple object turns out to be one of the most important tools in all of mathematics.

The unit circle: $x^2 + y^2 = 1$

Every point on this circle is exactly 1 unit away from the origin. If we label a point on the circle with coordinates $(x, y)$, then we know that $x^2 + y^2 = 1$ by the Pythagorean theorem (since the radius is 1).

Fig. 3.2-1: The Unit Circle — Basic Setup
The unit circle centered at the origin with radius 1. Any point P on the circle has coordinates (x, y) where x² + y² = 1.

Connecting the Unit Circle to Trigonometry

Now here's the key insight: let's measure an angle $\theta$ starting from the positive x-axis and rotating counterclockwise. Draw a line from the origin at angle $\theta$, and mark where it intersects the unit circle. Call that point $P$.

What are the coordinates of point $P$? This is where trigonometry enters. If we drop a perpendicular from $P$ to the x-axis, we create a right triangle. In this triangle:

  • The hypotenuse is the radius, which equals 1
  • The horizontal leg (adjacent to angle $\theta$) has length $x$
  • The vertical leg (opposite to angle $\theta$) has length $y$

Using our right triangle definitions from Module 2:

$\cos \theta = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}} = \frac{x}{1} = x$

$\sin \theta = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}} = \frac{y}{1} = y$

This gives us a profound realization: On the unit circle, the coordinates of any point are simply $(\cos \theta, \sin \theta)$.

Unit Circle Definition of Trigonometric Functions:

For any angle $\theta$ (measured counterclockwise from the positive x-axis), the point where the terminal side of the angle intersects the unit circle has coordinates:

$(x, y) = (\cos \theta, \sin \theta)$
Fig. 3.2-2: Unit Circle with Angle θ
Moving the point P around the circle shows how cos θ and sin θ vary with the angle. The x-coordinate is always cos θ, and the y-coordinate is always sin θ.

This definition is incredibly powerful because:

  • It works for any angle: 30°, 150°, -45°, 720°—any angle at all
  • It's consistent with right triangles: For angles between 0° and 90°, it gives exactly the same values we learned in Module 2
  • It reveals patterns: The geometric nature of the circle shows us why certain relationships hold
  • It explains periodicity: Going around the circle multiple times shows why trig functions repeat

3.3 The Four Quadrants

The coordinate plane divides naturally into four quadrants, and angles in each quadrant have different sign patterns for sine and cosine.

Sign Patterns by Quadrant:

  • Quadrant I (0° to 90°): Both x and y are positive → cos and sin are both positive
  • Quadrant II (90° to 180°): x is negative, y is positive → cos is negative, sin is positive
  • Quadrant III (180° to 270°): Both x and y are negative → cos and sin are both negative
  • Quadrant IV (270° to 360°): x is positive, y is negative → cos is positive, sin is negative

3.4 Radian Measure: A Natural Way to Measure Angles

The Problem with Degrees

We're all familiar with measuring angles in degrees. A full circle is 360°, a right angle is 90°, and so on. But have you ever wondered why 360? It's a somewhat arbitrary choice—historically based on ancient Babylonian astronomy and their base-60 number system.

In advanced mathematics, physics, and engineering, we use a more natural unit: the radian. Radians arise directly from the geometry of the circle itself, making many formulas simpler and more elegant.

What Is a Radian?

Definition of Radian:

One radian is the angle subtended at the center of a circle by an arc whose length equals the radius of the circle.

Fig. 3.4-1: One Radian — Arc Length Equals Radius
When the arc length equals the radius r, the angle at the center is exactly 1 radian. This is approximately 57.3°.

Think of it this way: imagine taking the radius of a circle and "wrapping" it along the circle's circumference. The angle you sweep out at the center is one radian.

Why Radians?

Radians make many mathematical relationships cleaner. For instance:

  • The arc length formula becomes simply: $s = r\theta$ (where $\theta$ is in radians)
  • In calculus, derivatives of trig functions work out cleanly only with radians
  • Many physics formulas (angular velocity, simple harmonic motion) are simpler in radians

Converting Between Degrees and Radians

Since the circumference of a circle is $2\pi r$, and this corresponds to 360°, we have:

$2\pi$ radians = 360°
$\pi$ radians = 180°

This gives us the conversion formulas:

Degree-Radian Conversions:

$\text{radians} = \text{degrees} \times \frac{\pi}{180}$

$\text{degrees} = \text{radians} \times \frac{180}{\pi}$

Example Conversions:

  • 90° = $\frac{\pi}{2}$ radians (quarter circle)
  • 45° = $\frac{\pi}{4}$ radians
  • 60° = $\frac{\pi}{3}$ radians
  • 30° = $\frac{\pi}{6}$ radians
  • 180° = $\pi$ radians (half circle)
  • 360° = $2\pi$ radians (full circle)

Historical Note: The radian as a formal unit of angular measure was introduced in the 1870s, though the underlying concept of measuring angles by arc length was known much earlier. The term "radian" comes from the Latin word "radius."

While radians might seem less intuitive at first than degrees, they're actually more fundamental—they arise naturally from the circle's geometry without any arbitrary choices. This is why radians dominate in higher mathematics and theoretical physics.

3.5 Reference Angles and Sign Patterns

The Concept of Reference Angles

When working with angles beyond the first quadrant, we often use reference angles to simplify calculations. The reference angle is the acute angle (0 to 90°, or 0 to π/2 radians) that the terminal side of your angle makes with the x-axis.

Finding Reference Angles:

  • Quadrant I: Reference angle = angle itself
  • Quadrant II: Reference angle = 180° - angle (or π - angle)
  • Quadrant III: Reference angle = angle - 180° (or angle - π)
  • Quadrant IV: Reference angle = 360° - angle (or 2π - angle)

Example: Find sin(150°)

Solution:

  • 150° is in Quadrant II
  • Reference angle = 180° - 150° = 30°
  • In Quadrant II, sine is positive
  • So sin(150°) = +sin(30°) = 1/2

The "All Students Take Calculus" Mnemonic

A helpful way to remember which trig functions are positive in each quadrant:

  • All (Quadrant I): All functions positive
  • Students (Quadrant II): Only Sine positive
  • Take (Quadrant III): Only Tangent positive
  • Calculus (Quadrant IV): Only Cosine positive

The "ASTC" rule helps remember which functions are positive in each quadrant.

3.6 Special Angles and Their Values

Certain angles appear so frequently in mathematics and applications that it's worth memorizing their sine and cosine values. These are typically called the "special angles."

The 30-60-90 Triangle

From a 30-60-90 right triangle with hypotenuse 1:

  • cos(30°) = √3/2, sin(30°) = 1/2
  • cos(60°) = 1/2, sin(60°) = √3/2

The 45-45-90 Triangle

From a 45-45-90 right triangle with hypotenuse 1:

  • cos(45°) = √2/2, sin(45°) = √2/2

Axis Angles

When the angle points directly along an axis:

  • 0° (or 0 radians): cos(0) = 1, sin(0) = 0
  • 90° (or π/2): cos(90°) = 0, sin(90°) = 1
  • 180° (or π): cos(180°) = -1, sin(180°) = 0
  • 270° (or 3π/2): cos(270°) = 0, sin(270°) = -1
Fig. 3.6-1: Complete Unit Circle with All Special Angles
All the special angles with their radian measures and coordinate pairs (cos θ, sin θ). This diagram is a reference you'll use throughout your study of trigonometry.

Memorization Strategy: Rather than memorizing all these values separately, notice the patterns:

  • The values involve 0, 1/2, √2/2, √3/2, and 1
  • As you go counterclockwise around the circle, sine increases from 0 to 1 in the first quadrant, then decreases back to 0 in the second
  • Cosine decreases from 1 to 0 in the first quadrant, then becomes negative in the second
  • Angles that differ by 90° swap sine and cosine values (with possible sign changes)

3.7 Arc Length and Sector Area

Arc Length Formula

One of the most elegant benefits of radian measure is the simplicity of the arc length formula. If you have a circle of radius $r$ and an angle $\theta$ (in radians), the length of the arc subtended by that angle is:

$s = r\theta$

That's it! Just radius times angle. Compare this to the degree version:

$s = \frac{\theta}{360} \times 2\pi r$ (when $\theta$ is in degrees)

The radian formula is clearly simpler—this is one of the key reasons radians are preferred in mathematics.

Fig. 3.7-1: Arc Length — s = rθ
The arc length s is simply the radius r times the angle θ (in radians).

Example: A circle has radius 5 cm. What is the arc length for an angle of π/3 radians (60°)?

Solution:

Using $s = r\theta$:

$s = 5 \times \frac{\pi}{3} = \frac{5\pi}{3}$ cm ≈ 5.24 cm

Sector Area Formula

A sector is a "pie slice" of a circle—the region bounded by two radii and the arc between them. The area of a sector with angle $\theta$ (in radians) and radius $r$ is:

$A = \frac{1}{2}r^2\theta$

This formula has a beautiful interpretation: it's halfway between the area of a triangle ($\frac{1}{2}bh$) and the area of a circle ($\pi r^2$). Indeed, if $\theta = 2\pi$ (a full circle), we get $A = \frac{1}{2}r^2(2\pi) = \pi r^2$, exactly right!

Example: Find the area of a sector with radius 4 meters and angle π/4 radians (45°).

Solution:

Using $A = \frac{1}{2}r^2\theta$:

$A = \frac{1}{2}(4)^2\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right) = \frac{1}{2}(16)\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right) = 2\pi$ square meters ≈ 6.28 m²

3.8 Practice Problems

Problem 1: Convert the following angles from degrees to radians:

  1. 45°
  2. 135°
  3. 270°
  4. -60°

Solution:

Use the formula: radians = degrees × (π/180)

  1. 45° = 45 × (π/180) = π/4 radians
  2. 135° = 135 × (π/180) = 3π/4 radians
  3. 270° = 270 × (π/180) = 3π/2 radians
  4. -60° = -60 × (π/180) = -π/3 radians

Problem 2: Convert the following angles from radians to degrees:

  1. π/6
  2. 2π/3
  3. 5π/4
  4. -π/2

Solution:

Use the formula: degrees = radians × (180/π)

  1. π/6 = (π/6) × (180/π) = 30°
  2. 2π/3 = (2π/3) × (180/π) = 120°
  3. 5π/4 = (5π/4) × (180/π) = 225°
  4. -π/2 = (-π/2) × (180/π) = -90°

Problem 3: Without using a calculator, find the exact values:

  1. sin(120°)
  2. cos(225°)
  3. sin(5π/6)
  4. cos(7π/4)

Solution:

  1. 120° is in Quadrant II. Reference angle: 180° - 120° = 60°. Sine is positive in Q2. sin(120°) = sin(60°) = √3/2
  2. 225° is in Quadrant III. Reference angle: 225° - 180° = 45°. Cosine is negative in Q3. cos(225°) = -cos(45°) = -√2/2
  3. 5π/6 = 150° is in Quadrant II. Reference angle: π - 5π/6 = π/6 (30°). Sine is positive in Q2. sin(5π/6) = sin(π/6) = 1/2
  4. 7π/4 = 315° is in Quadrant IV. Reference angle: 2π - 7π/4 = π/4 (45°). Cosine is positive in Q4. cos(7π/4) = cos(π/4) = √2/2

Problem 4: A wheel of radius 30 cm rotates through an angle of 2.5 radians. How far does a point on the rim of the wheel travel?

Solution:

Use the arc length formula: s = rθ

s = (30 cm)(2.5 radians) = 75 cm

The point travels 75 centimeters.

Problem 5: Find the area of a sector with radius 6 inches and central angle π/3 radians (60°).

Solution:

Use the sector area formula: A = (1/2)r²θ

A = (1/2)(6)²(π/3) = (1/2)(36)(π/3) = 6π square inches ≈ 18.85 in²

Problem 6: If sin(θ) = 0.6 and θ is in Quadrant II, find cos(θ) and tan(θ).

Solution:

We know sin(θ) = y = 0.6 on the unit circle, and we need to find x = cos(θ).

Using x² + y² = 1:

x² + (0.6)² = 1

x² + 0.36 = 1

x² = 0.64

x = ±0.8

Since θ is in Quadrant II, x (and therefore cos θ) must be negative:

cos(θ) = -0.8

tan(θ) = sin(θ)/cos(θ) = 0.6/(-0.8) = -0.75

3.9 Summary

In this module, we've extended trigonometry from right triangles to all angles using the unit circle:

  • The unit circle provides a universal definition: for any angle θ, the point where the terminal side intersects the circle has coordinates (cos θ, sin θ)
  • Quadrants determine the signs of trig functions based on whether x and y coordinates are positive or negative
  • Radians are a more natural unit than degrees, arising from the circle's geometry itself, and making many formulas simpler
  • Reference angles help us reduce calculations for any angle to familiar acute angles
  • Special angles (30°, 45°, 60°, and multiples) have exact, memorable values that appear constantly in applications
  • Arc length (s = rθ) and sector area (A = ½r²θ) formulas are elegantly simple when using radians

The unit circle is more than just a computational tool—it's a window into the periodic, oscillating nature of trigonometric functions. In the next modules, we'll explore these periodic behaviors, learn about graphs of trig functions, develop important identities, and see how these ideas apply to real-world problems in physics, engineering, and beyond.
