In the previous modules, we've learned how to calculate trigonometric functions for various angles, interpret them geometrically using the unit circle, and visualize their periodic behavior through graphs. We've seen that these functions have specific values at certain angles—for instance, $\sin 30° = 1/2$ and $\cos 45° = \sqrt{2}/2$. But trigonometry reveals something deeper: there are fundamental relationships between these functions that are always true, regardless of the angle.

These relationships are called trigonometric identities. Unlike equations that are true only for specific values of the variable, identities are true for all values where both sides are defined. They're not just mathematical curiosities—they're essential tools that mathematicians, scientists, and engineers use daily to simplify expressions, solve equations, and reveal hidden connections.

5.1 What Are Identities and Why Do They Matter?

Understanding Identities

An identity is an equation that is true for all values of the variable(s) involved. For example, the algebraic identity $(x + y)^2 = x^2 + 2xy + y^2$ is true regardless of what numbers $x$ and $y$ represent. Similarly, trigonometric identities express relationships that hold for all angles (or at least for all angles where the functions are defined).

Example: Comparing Equations and Identities

Equation: $\sin \theta = 0.5$ is true only for specific angles like $\theta = 30°, 150°, 390°$, etc.

Identity: $\sin^2 \theta + \cos^2 \theta = 1$ is true for every angle $\theta$.

Why Identities Matter: Three Compelling Reasons

1. Simplification and Problem Solving

Identities let us transform complicated trigonometric expressions into simpler, more manageable forms. In calculus, for instance, you might need to integrate an expression like $\sin^2 x$. Direct integration is difficult, but using the identity $\sin^2 x = \frac{1 - \cos 2x}{2}$ transforms it into something readily integrable. Engineers use identities constantly to simplify formulas describing waves, vibrations, and alternating currents.

2. Revealing Mathematical Structure

Identities show us that seemingly different expressions are actually the same thing in disguise. This reveals deep connections in mathematics. For example, the sum formula $\sin(A + B) = \sin A \cos B + \cos A \sin B$ shows how the sine of a sum relates to the sines and cosines of individual angles. This isn't just a computational trick—it reflects fundamental properties of rotation and periodic motion.

3. Historical Context: The Development of Trigonometric Identities

The systematic study of trigonometric identities spans cultures and centuries. Greek astronomers like Ptolemy (c. 100-170 CE) knew some basic relationships, but the golden age of identity development came during the Islamic Golden Age (800-1400 CE).

Abu al-Wafa (940-998 CE), a Persian mathematician and astronomer working in Baghdad, made groundbreaking contributions to trigonometry. He was the first to systematically develop the tangent function and discover many angle addition formulas. Abu al-Wafa was particularly concerned with accurate astronomical calculations, and his work on identities made complex computations more tractable. His treatise Almagest of Abu al-Wafa contains some of the earliest derivations of sum and difference formulas.

Leonhard Euler (1707-1783) later brought modern notation and systematic organization to trigonometry. In his monumental Introduction to the Analysis of the Infinite (1748), Euler established the notation and relationships we use today, connecting trigonometric functions with complex numbers and exponential functions through his famous formula $e^{i\theta} = \cos \theta + i \sin \theta$.

5.2 The Pythagorean Identities

The Fundamental Identity

The most important trigonometric identity comes directly from the Pythagorean theorem. Recall from Module 3 that any point on the unit circle has coordinates $(\cos \theta, \sin \theta)$, where $\theta$ is the angle from the positive x-axis. Since the point lies on a circle of radius 1, the distance from the origin to the point equals 1.

Using the distance formula (which is just the Pythagorean theorem):

$\sqrt{(\cos \theta)^2 + (\sin \theta)^2} = 1$

Squaring both sides gives us:

$\sin^2 \theta + \cos^2 \theta = 1$

The Fundamental Pythagorean Identity

$\sin^2 \theta + \cos^2 \theta = 1$

This identity is true for every angle $\theta$. It's called a Pythagorean identity because it comes from the Pythagorean theorem applied to the unit circle.

Fig. 5.2-1: Geometric Proof of the Pythagorean Identity
On the unit circle, a point at angle θ has coordinates (cos θ, sin θ). By the Pythagorean theorem, cos²θ + sin²θ equals the square of the radius, which is 1.

Two Related Pythagorean Identities

From the fundamental identity, we can derive two more Pythagorean identities by dividing through by $\cos^2 \theta$ or $\sin^2 \theta$.

Dividing by $\cos^2 \theta$:

Starting with $\sin^2 \theta + \cos^2 \theta = 1$, divide every term by $\cos^2 \theta$:

$\frac{\sin^2 \theta}{\cos^2 \theta} + \frac{\cos^2 \theta}{\cos^2 \theta} = \frac{1}{\cos^2 \theta}$

Using the definitions $\tan \theta = \frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta}$ and $\sec \theta = \frac{1}{\cos \theta}$:

$\tan^2 \theta + 1 = \sec^2 \theta$

Or, rearranging:

$1 + \tan^2 \theta = \sec^2 \theta$

Dividing by $\sin^2 \theta$:

Similarly, dividing the fundamental identity by $\sin^2 \theta$:

$1 + \cot^2 \theta = \csc^2 \theta$

The Three Pythagorean Identities

  1. $\sin^2 \theta + \cos^2 \theta = 1$
  2. $1 + \tan^2 \theta = \sec^2 \theta$
  3. $1 + \cot^2 \theta = \csc^2 \theta$

These three identities are among the most frequently used in trigonometry. Memorize them—they're worth it!

Using the Pythagorean Identities

Example 1: Finding $\cos \theta$ given $\sin \theta$

If $\sin \theta = 3/5$ and $\theta$ is in Quadrant I, find $\cos \theta$.

Solution:

Using $\sin^2 \theta + \cos^2 \theta = 1$:

$(3/5)^2 + \cos^2 \theta = 1$

$9/25 + \cos^2 \theta = 1$

$\cos^2 \theta = 1 - 9/25 = 16/25$

$\cos \theta = \pm 4/5$

Since $\theta$ is in Quadrant I where cosine is positive: $\cos \theta = 4/5$

Example 2: Simplifying an Expression

Simplify: $\frac{1 - \sin^2 \theta}{\cos \theta}$

Solution:

From the Pythagorean identity, we know $1 - \sin^2 \theta = \cos^2 \theta$:

$\frac{1 - \sin^2 \theta}{\cos \theta} = \frac{\cos^2 \theta}{\cos \theta} = \cos \theta$

Practice Problem 1: If $\cos \theta = -5/13$ and $\theta$ is in Quadrant II, find $\sin \theta$ and $\tan \theta$.
Solution:
Using $\sin^2 \theta + \cos^2 \theta = 1$:
$\sin^2 \theta + (-5/13)^2 = 1$
$\sin^2 \theta = 1 - 25/169 = 144/169$
$\sin \theta = \pm 12/13$
In Quadrant II, sine is positive, so $\sin \theta = 12/13$
Then $\tan \theta = \frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta} = \frac{12/13}{-5/13} = -\frac{12}{5}$
Practice Problem 2: Simplify: $\sec^2 x - \tan^2 x$
Solution:
Using the identity $1 + \tan^2 x = \sec^2 x$:
$\sec^2 x - \tan^2 x = 1$

5.3 Reciprocal and Quotient Identities

These identities follow directly from the definitions of the trigonometric functions. They're straightforward but essential for converting between functions.

Reciprocal Identities

$\csc \theta = \frac{1}{\sin \theta}$     $\sec \theta = \frac{1}{\cos \theta}$     $\cot \theta = \frac{1}{\tan \theta}$

Quotient Identities

$\tan \theta = \frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta}$      $\cot \theta = \frac{\cos \theta}{\sin \theta}$

These might seem obvious, but they're incredibly useful for transforming expressions. Often, converting everything to sines and cosines using these identities makes simplification much easier.

Example: Simplifying Using Quotient Identities

Simplify: $\tan \theta \cos \theta$

Solution:

$\tan \theta \cos \theta = \frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta} \cdot \cos \theta = \sin \theta$

5.4 Even-Odd Identities

A function $f$ is termed even if $f(x) = f(-x)$ for all $x$ in its domain, and odd if $f(x) = -f(-x)$ for all $x$ in its domain. Even functions are symmetric about the y-axis, while odd functions are symmetric about the origin. The trigonometric functions have specific even-odd properties that lead to important identities.

Visualizing Even and Odd Functions

Before we look at the trigonometric functions specifically, let's understand what even and odd symmetry looks like graphically.

Fig. 5.4-1: Example of an Even Function: f(x) = x²
An even function is symmetric about the y-axis. Notice that f(-2) = f(2) = 4, and more generally f(-x) = f(x) for all x.
Fig. 5.4-2: Example of an Odd Function: f(x) = x³
An odd function is symmetric about the origin. Notice that f(-2) = -f(2): if (2, 8) is on the graph, then (-2, -8) is also on the graph.

Visualizing Symmetry on the Unit Circle

Consider an angle $\theta$ and its negative $-\theta$. On the unit circle, $-\theta$ is the reflection of $\theta$ across the x-axis. The point at angle $\theta$ has coordinates $(\cos \theta, \sin \theta)$, while the point at angle $-\theta$ has coordinates $(\cos(-\theta), \sin(-\theta))$.

Fig. 5.4-3: Even and Odd Behavior on the Unit Circle
Comparing points at angle θ and -θ on the unit circle. Notice that cos(-θ) = cos(θ) (x-coordinates match), but sin(-θ) = -sin(θ) (y-coordinates are opposites).

From the diagram, we can see:

  • The x-coordinates are the same: $\cos(-\theta) = \cos \theta$
  • The y-coordinates are opposites: $\sin(-\theta) = -\sin \theta$

Even-Odd Identities

Cosine is an even function:

$\cos(-\theta) = \cos \theta$

Sine is an odd function:

$\sin(-\theta) = -\sin \theta$

Tangent is an odd function:

$\tan(-\theta) = -\tan \theta$

(The tangent identity follows from the sine and cosine identities: $\tan(-\theta) = \frac{\sin(-\theta)}{\cos(-\theta)} = \frac{-\sin \theta}{\cos \theta} = -\tan \theta$)

Example: Using Even-Odd Identities

Simplify: $\cos(-45°) + \sin(-30°)$

Solution:

Using the even-odd identities:

$\cos(-45°) + \sin(-30°) = \cos(45°) + (-\sin(30°))$

$= \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} - \frac{1}{2}$

$= \frac{\sqrt{2} - 1}{2}$

Practice Problem 3: If $\sin \theta = 0.6$, what is $\sin(-\theta)$?
Solution:
Using the odd function property of sine:
$\sin(-\theta) = -\sin \theta = -0.6$

5.5 Cofunction Identities

There's an interesting relationship between sine and cosine, and between tangent and cotangent, that involves complementary angles—angles that add up to 90°.

The Complementary Angle Connection

In a right triangle, if one of the acute angles is $\theta$, then the other acute angle must be $90° - \theta$ (since the three angles must sum to 180°, and one angle is already 90°). The side that is "opposite" to angle $\theta$ is "adjacent" to angle $90° - \theta$, and vice versa.

This geometric relationship leads to a beautiful connection:

$\sin \theta = \cos(90° - \theta)$

$\cos \theta = \sin(90° - \theta)$

In other words, the sine of an angle equals the cosine of its complement—which is exactly why "cosine" is called "cosine": it's the sine of the complementary angle!

Etymology: Why "Cosine," "Cotangent," and "Cosecant"?

The "co-" prefix in cosine, cotangent, and cosecant literally means "complement's." When early mathematicians organized these functions, they recognized that:

  • The cosine of an angle $A$ is equal to the sine of the angle complementary to $A$
  • The cotangent of an angle $A$ is equal to the tangent of the angle complementary to $A$
  • The cosecant of an angle $A$ is equal to the secant of the angle complementary to $A$

This naming convention, which seems arbitrary at first, actually encodes a fundamental geometric relationship. The term "cosinus" appeared in Edmund Gunter's Canon triangulorum (1620), though the relationship was understood much earlier by Islamic mathematicians.

Cofunction Identities (in degrees)

$\sin \theta = \cos(90° - \theta)$     $\cos \theta = \sin(90° - \theta)$

$\tan \theta = \cot(90° - \theta)$     $\cot \theta = \tan(90° - \theta)$

$\sec \theta = \csc(90° - \theta)$     $\csc \theta = \sec(90° - \theta)$

Cofunction Identities (in radians)

$\sin \theta = \cos(\frac{\pi}{2} - \theta)$     $\cos \theta = \sin(\frac{\pi}{2} - \theta)$

(and similarly for the other functions)

Example: Using Cofunction Identities

Find $\sin 70°$ if you know that $\cos 20° \approx 0.9397$.

Solution:

Since $70° = 90° - 20°$, we can use the cofunction identity:

$\sin 70° = \cos(90° - 70°) = \cos 20° \approx 0.9397$

Practice Problem 4: Simplify: $\sin(90° - x) \cos x + \cos(90° - x) \sin x$
Solution:
Using cofunction identities: $\sin(90° - x) = \cos x$ and $\cos(90° - x) = \sin x$
$\cos x \cdot \cos x + \sin x \cdot \sin x = \cos^2 x + \sin^2 x = 1$

5.6 Sum and Difference Formulas

One of the most powerful sets of identities tells us how to find the sine, cosine, or tangent of a sum or difference of angles. These formulas might seem complicated at first, but they're incredibly useful and have elegant geometric derivations.

The Cosine of a Difference

Let's start with the formula for $\cos(A - B)$. While the full geometric proof is intricate, we can understand the key insight through a diagram. Consider two angles $A$ and $B$ on the unit circle, and think about the angle between them, which is $A - B$.

Fig. 5.6-1: Geometric Intuition for cos(A - B)
Two points on the unit circle at angles A and B. The distance between them depends on the angle difference A - B, leading to the cosine difference formula.

Through careful geometric analysis (using the distance formula for points on the unit circle), we arrive at:

$\cos(A - B) = \cos A \cos B + \sin A \sin B$

From this fundamental formula, we can derive all the other sum and difference formulas by clever substitutions.

Deriving the Other Sum and Difference Formulas

Cosine of a sum: Replace $B$ with $-B$ in the difference formula and use even-odd identities:

$\cos(A + B) = \cos(A - (-B)) = \cos A \cos(-B) + \sin A \sin(-B)$
$= \cos A \cos B - \sin A \sin B$

Sine of a sum: Use the cofunction identity $\sin \theta = \cos(90° - \theta)$:

$\sin(A + B) = \cos(90° - (A + B)) = \cos((90° - A) - B)$
$= \cos(90° - A)\cos B + \sin(90° - A)\sin B$
$= \sin A \cos B + \cos A \sin B$

Sine of a difference: Replace $B$ with $-B$:

$\sin(A - B) = \sin A \cos B - \cos A \sin B$

Sum and Difference Formulas

Cosine:

$\cos(A + B) = \cos A \cos B - \sin A \sin B$
$\cos(A - B) = \cos A \cos B + \sin A \sin B$

Sine:

$\sin(A + B) = \sin A \cos B + \cos A \sin B$
$\sin(A - B) = \sin A \cos B - \cos A \sin B$

Tangent:

$\tan(A + B) = \frac{\tan A + \tan B}{1 - \tan A \tan B}$
$\tan(A - B) = \frac{\tan A - \tan B}{1 + \tan A \tan B}$

Common Mistake Alert!

It is NOT true that $\sin(A + B) = \sin A + \sin B$. The sine of a sum is not the sum of the sines!

For example: $\sin(30° + 60°) = \sin 90° = 1$, but $\sin 30° + \sin 60° = 0.5 + 0.866 = 1.366 \neq 1$

Using Sum and Difference Formulas

Example 1: Finding Exact Values

Find the exact value of $\sin 75°$ without a calculator.

Solution:

Notice that $75° = 45° + 30°$, and we know the exact values for these angles.

Using $\sin(A + B) = \sin A \cos B + \cos A \sin B$:

$\sin 75° = \sin(45° + 30°)$
$= \sin 45° \cos 30° + \cos 45° \sin 30°$
$= \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} + \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2}$
$= \frac{\sqrt{6}}{4} + \frac{\sqrt{2}}{4} = \frac{\sqrt{6} + \sqrt{2}}{4}$

Example 2: Simplifying Expressions

Simplify: $\sin x \cos 2x + \cos x \sin 2x$

Solution:

This matches the pattern of $\sin(A + B) = \sin A \cos B + \cos A \sin B$ with $A = x$ and $B = 2x$:

$\sin x \cos 2x + \cos x \sin 2x = \sin(x + 2x) = \sin 3x$

Practice Problem 5: Find the exact value of $\cos 15°$ using a difference formula.
Solution:
Let $15° = 45° - 30°$
Using $\cos(A - B) = \cos A \cos B + \sin A \sin B$:
$\cos 15° = \cos(45° - 30°) = \cos 45° \cos 30° + \sin 45° \sin 30°$
$= \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} + \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2}$
$= \frac{\sqrt{6}}{4} + \frac{\sqrt{2}}{4} = \frac{\sqrt{6} + \sqrt{2}}{4}$
Practice Problem 6: Prove that $\cos(180° - \theta) = -\cos \theta$
Solution:
Using the cosine difference formula:
$\cos(180° - \theta) = \cos 180° \cos \theta + \sin 180° \sin \theta$
$= (-1)(\cos \theta) + (0)(\sin \theta) = -\cos \theta$

5.7 Double Angle Formulas

A special case of the sum formulas occurs when both angles are the same: $A = B$. These "double angle formulas" appear frequently in calculus, physics, and engineering.

Deriving Double Angle Formulas

Starting with the sine sum formula and setting $A = B = \theta$:

$\sin(2\theta) = \sin(\theta + \theta) = \sin \theta \cos \theta + \cos \theta \sin \theta$
$= 2\sin \theta \cos \theta$

For cosine, starting with the cosine sum formula:

$\cos(2\theta) = \cos(\theta + \theta) = \cos \theta \cos \theta - \sin \theta \sin \theta$
$= \cos^2 \theta - \sin^2 \theta$

This formula for $\cos(2\theta)$ can be rewritten in two other useful forms using the Pythagorean identity $\sin^2 \theta + \cos^2 \theta = 1$:

Form 2: Replace $\sin^2 \theta$ with $1 - \cos^2 \theta$:

$\cos(2\theta) = \cos^2 \theta - (1 - \cos^2 \theta) = 2\cos^2 \theta - 1$

Form 3: Replace $\cos^2 \theta$ with $1 - \sin^2 \theta$:

$\cos(2\theta) = (1 - \sin^2 \theta) - \sin^2 \theta = 1 - 2\sin^2 \theta$

Double Angle Formulas

$\sin(2\theta) = 2\sin \theta \cos \theta$

$\cos(2\theta) = \cos^2 \theta - \sin^2 \theta$
$\cos(2\theta) = 2\cos^2 \theta - 1$
$\cos(2\theta) = 1 - 2\sin^2 \theta$

$\tan(2\theta) = \frac{2\tan \theta}{1 - \tan^2 \theta}$

The three forms of the cosine double angle formula are all equivalent—use whichever is most convenient for the problem at hand.

Applications of Double Angle Formulas

Example 1: Calculating Double Angles

If $\sin \theta = 3/5$ and $\theta$ is in Quadrant I, find $\sin(2\theta)$ and $\cos(2\theta)$.

Solution:

First, find $\cos \theta$ using the Pythagorean identity:

$\cos^2 \theta = 1 - \sin^2 \theta = 1 - 9/25 = 16/25$

Since $\theta$ is in Quadrant I: $\cos \theta = 4/5$

Now use the double angle formulas:

$\sin(2\theta) = 2\sin \theta \cos \theta = 2 \cdot \frac{3}{5} \cdot \frac{4}{5} = \frac{24}{25}$

$\cos(2\theta) = \cos^2 \theta - \sin^2 \theta = \frac{16}{25} - \frac{9}{25} = \frac{7}{25}$

Example 2: Simplifying with Double Angle Formulas

Express $\sin^2 \theta$ in terms of $\cos(2\theta)$.

Solution:

Start with $\cos(2\theta) = 1 - 2\sin^2 \theta$ and solve for $\sin^2 \theta$:

$2\sin^2 \theta = 1 - \cos(2\theta)$

$\sin^2 \theta = \frac{1 - \cos(2\theta)}{2}$

This identity is extremely useful in calculus for integrating $\sin^2 \theta$!

Fig. 5.7-1: Visualizing sin(2θ) = 2sin(θ)cos(θ)
The graph shows sin(θ) (blue), sin(2θ) (red), and 2sin(θ)cos(θ) (green dashed). Since by the double angle formula sin(2θ) = 2sin(θ)cos(θ), those two curves completely coincide, resulting in the single red/green curve shown.
Practice Problem 7: If $\cos \theta = -4/5$ and $\theta$ is in Quadrant II, find $\sin(2\theta)$.
Solution:
First find $\sin \theta$: $\sin^2 \theta = 1 - \cos^2 \theta = 1 - 16/25 = 9/25$
In Quadrant II, sine is positive: $\sin \theta = 3/5$
$\sin(2\theta) = 2\sin \theta \cos \theta = 2 \cdot \frac{3}{5} \cdot \left(-\frac{4}{5}\right) = -\frac{24}{25}$

5.8 Half Angle Formulas

The half angle formulas allow us to express trigonometric functions of $\theta/2$ in terms of $\theta$. They're derived directly from the double angle formulas by a clever substitution.

Deriving the Half Angle Formulas

Start with the double angle formula $\cos(2\alpha) = 1 - 2\sin^2 \alpha$. Let $\alpha = \theta/2$, so $2\alpha = \theta$:

$\cos \theta = 1 - 2\sin^2(\theta/2)$

Solving for $\sin(\theta/2)$:

$2\sin^2(\theta/2) = 1 - \cos \theta$
$\sin^2(\theta/2) = \frac{1 - \cos \theta}{2}$
$\sin(\theta/2) = \pm\sqrt{\frac{1 - \cos \theta}{2}}$

Similarly, using $\cos(2\alpha) = 2\cos^2 \alpha - 1$:

$\cos(\theta/2) = \pm\sqrt{\frac{1 + \cos \theta}{2}}$

Half Angle Formulas

$\sin(\theta/2) = \pm\sqrt{\frac{1 - \cos \theta}{2}}$

$\cos(\theta/2) = \pm\sqrt{\frac{1 + \cos \theta}{2}}$

$\tan(\theta/2) = \frac{\sin \theta}{1 + \cos \theta} = \frac{1 - \cos \theta}{\sin \theta}$

The sign (+ or -) depends on which quadrant $\theta/2$ lies in.

Example: Using Half Angle Formulas

Find the exact value of $\sin 22.5°$.

Solution:

Notice that $22.5° = 45°/2$, so we can use the half angle formula with $\theta = 45°$:

$\sin 22.5° = \sin(45°/2) = \sqrt{\frac{1 - \cos 45°}{2}}$

(We use the positive square root since $22.5°$ is in Quadrant I)

$= \sqrt{\frac{1 - \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}}{2}} = \sqrt{\frac{2 - \sqrt{2}}{4}} = \frac{\sqrt{2 - \sqrt{2}}}{2}$

Practice Problem 8: Find $\cos 15°$ using a half angle formula.
Solution:
Since $15° = 30°/2$, use $\cos(\theta/2) = \sqrt{\frac{1 + \cos \theta}{2}}$ with $\theta = 30°$:
$\cos 15° = \sqrt{\frac{1 + \cos 30°}{2}} = \sqrt{\frac{1 + \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}}{2}} = \sqrt{\frac{2 + \sqrt{3}}{4}} = \frac{\sqrt{2 + \sqrt{3}}}{2}$

5.9 Product-to-Sum and Sum-to-Product Formulas

The Product-to-Sum Formulas

These formulas are derived by adding or subtracting the sum and difference formulas. For example, adding $\sin(A + B) = \sin A \cos B + \cos A \sin B$ and $\sin(A - B) = \sin A \cos B - \cos A \sin B$ gives:

$\sin(A + B) + \sin(A - B) = 2\sin A \cos B$

Rearranging:

$\sin A \cos B = \frac{1}{2}[\sin(A + B) + \sin(A - B)]$

Product-to-Sum Formulas

$\sin A \cos B = \frac{1}{2}[\sin(A + B) + \sin(A - B)]$

$\cos A \sin B = \frac{1}{2}[\sin(A + B) - \sin(A - B)]$

$\cos A \cos B = \frac{1}{2}[\cos(A + B) + \cos(A - B)]$

$\sin A \sin B = \frac{1}{2}[\cos(A - B) - \cos(A + B)]$

Sum-to-Product Formulas

By substituting $A = (x + y)/2$ and $B = (x - y)/2$ into the product-to-sum formulas, we get the sum-to-product formulas:

Sum-to-Product Formulas

$\sin x + \sin y = 2\sin\left(\frac{x + y}{2}\right)\cos\left(\frac{x - y}{2}\right)$

$\sin x - \sin y = 2\cos\left(\frac{x + y}{2}\right)\sin\left(\frac{x - y}{2}\right)$

$\cos x + \cos y = 2\cos\left(\frac{x + y}{2}\right)\cos\left(\frac{x - y}{2}\right)$

$\cos x - \cos y = -2\sin\left(\frac{x + y}{2}\right)\sin\left(\frac{x - y}{2}\right)$

Modern Applications

The sum-to-product formulas remain important in modern applications, particularly in signal processing. When analyzing audio or radio signals, engineers often need to understand what happens when waves of different frequencies interact. The sum-to-product formulas explain phenomena like:

  • Beats: When two sound waves of slightly different frequencies combine, you hear a periodic variation in loudness (the "beat frequency")
  • Amplitude modulation (AM radio): Information is encoded by varying the amplitude of a carrier wave
  • Heterodyning: Converting high-frequency signals to lower frequencies for easier processing
Practice Problem 9: Express $\sin 5x \cos 3x$ as a sum or difference.
Solution:
Using the product-to-sum formula $\sin A \cos B = \frac{1}{2}[\sin(A + B) + \sin(A - B)]$:
$\sin 5x \cos 3x = \frac{1}{2}[\sin(5x + 3x) + \sin(5x - 3x)]$
$= \frac{1}{2}[\sin 8x + \sin 2x]$

5.10 Solving Trigonometric Equations

Now that we have a toolkit of identities, we can tackle trigonometric equations—equations where the unknown appears inside trigonometric functions. The strategy often involves using identities to simplify the equation to a form we can solve.

Basic Strategy for Solving Trigonometric Equations

  1. Isolate the trigonometric function if possible
  2. Use identities to simplify the equation, often converting to a single trigonometric function
  3. Solve for the angle using reference angles and the unit circle
  4. Find all solutions in the specified interval (or general solutions if no interval is specified)
  5. Check for extraneous solutions that might have been introduced during algebraic manipulation

Important: Trigonometric equations typically have infinitely many solutions because of periodicity. Unless the problem specifies an interval (like $0° \leq \theta < 360°$), you should give the general solution that accounts for all possible solutions.

Understanding General Solutions

Example 1: General Solution (All Solutions)

Solve $\sin \theta = \frac{1}{2}$ for all values of $\theta$.

Solution:

First, find solutions in one complete period $[0°, 360°)$:

  • $\theta = 30°$ (Quadrant I)
  • $\theta = 150°$ (Quadrant II)

Since sine has period $360°$, we can add any multiple of $360°$ to these solutions:

General solution: $\theta = 30° + 360°n$ or $\theta = 150° + 360°n$, where $n$ is any integer

In radians: $\theta = \frac{\pi}{6} + 2\pi n$ or $\theta = \frac{5\pi}{6} + 2\pi n$

This captures all infinitely many solutions: ..., -330°, -210°, 30°, 150°, 390°, 510°, ...

Example 2: General Solution for a Quadratic

Solve $\sin^2 \theta + \sin \theta - 2 = 0$ for all values of $\theta$.

Solution:

This is a quadratic equation in $\sin \theta$. Let $u = \sin \theta$:

$u^2 + u - 2 = 0$

Factor:

$(u + 2)(u - 1) = 0$

So $u = -2$ or $u = 1$

Since $\sin \theta = u$:

  • $\sin \theta = -2$ has no solution (sine values must be between -1 and 1)
  • $\sin \theta = 1$ gives $\theta = 90°$ in the first period

General solution: $\theta = 90° + 360°n$, where $n$ is any integer

In radians: $\theta = \frac{\pi}{2} + 2\pi n$

Type 1: Basic Equations with Interval Restrictions

When a problem specifies an interval, we find the general solution first, then select only those solutions that fall within the given interval.

Example 3: Solution in a Specified Interval

Solve $2\sin \theta - 1 = 0$ for $0° \leq \theta < 360°$.

Solution:

First, isolate $\sin \theta$:

$2\sin \theta = 1$

$\sin \theta = \frac{1}{2}$

From the unit circle, $\sin \theta = 1/2$ when:

  • $\theta = 30°$ (Quadrant I)
  • $\theta = 150°$ (Quadrant II)

Therefore: $\theta = 30°, 150°$

Example 4: Using Reference Angles

Solve $\cos \theta = -\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$ for $0 \leq \theta < 2\pi$.

Solution:

The reference angle is $\pi/6$ (where $\cos(\pi/6) = \sqrt{3}/2$).

Since cosine is negative in Quadrants II and III:

  • Quadrant II: $\theta = \pi - \pi/6 = 5\pi/6$
  • Quadrant III: $\theta = \pi + \pi/6 = 7\pi/6$

Therefore: $\theta = \frac{5\pi}{6}, \frac{7\pi}{6}$

Type 2: Equations Requiring Identities

Example 5: Converting to a Single Function

Solve $2\cos^2 \theta + \sin \theta - 1 = 0$ for $0° \leq \theta < 360°$.

Solution:

Use the Pythagorean identity $\cos^2 \theta = 1 - \sin^2 \theta$:

$2(1 - \sin^2 \theta) + \sin \theta - 1 = 0$

$2 - 2\sin^2 \theta + \sin \theta - 1 = 0$

$-2\sin^2 \theta + \sin \theta + 1 = 0$

Multiply by -1:

$2\sin^2 \theta - \sin \theta - 1 = 0$

Factor (or use quadratic formula):

$(2\sin \theta + 1)(\sin \theta - 1) = 0$

So $\sin \theta = -1/2$ or $\sin \theta = 1$

  • $\sin \theta = 1$: $\theta = 90°$
  • $\sin \theta = -1/2$: $\theta = 210°, 330°$ (Quadrants III and IV)

Therefore: $\theta = 90°, 210°, 330°$

Type 3: Equations with Multiple Angles

Example 6: Double Angle Equation

Solve $\sin(2\theta) = \frac{1}{2}$ for $0° \leq \theta < 360°$.

Solution:

Let $u = 2\theta$. Then we're solving $\sin u = 1/2$.

The general solution for $\sin u = 1/2$ is $u = 30° + 360°k$ or $u = 150° + 360°k$ where $k$ is any integer.

Since $u = 2\theta$:

$2\theta = 30° + 360°k$ or $2\theta = 150° + 360°k$

$\theta = 15° + 180°k$ or $\theta = 75° + 180°k$

For $0° \leq \theta < 360°$:

  • From $\theta = 15° + 180°k$: $\theta = 15°$ (k=0) and $\theta = 195°$ (k=1)
  • From $\theta = 75° + 180°k$: $\theta = 75°$ (k=0) and $\theta = 255°$ (k=1)

Therefore: $\theta = 15°, 75°, 195°, 255°$

Example 7: Using Double Angle Formula

Solve $\cos(2x) = \sin x$ for $0 \leq x < 2\pi$.

Solution:

Use the double angle formula $\cos(2x) = 1 - 2\sin^2 x$:

$1 - 2\sin^2 x = \sin x$

$2\sin^2 x + \sin x - 1 = 0$

Factor:

$(2\sin x - 1)(\sin x + 1) = 0$

So $\sin x = 1/2$ or $\sin x = -1$

  • $\sin x = 1/2$: $x = \pi/6, 5\pi/6$
  • $\sin x = -1$: $x = 3\pi/2$

Therefore: $x = \frac{\pi}{6}, \frac{5\pi}{6}, \frac{3\pi}{2}$

Type 4: Equations Requiring Factoring

Example 8: Factoring by Grouping

Solve $\sin \theta \cos \theta = \sin \theta$ for $0° \leq \theta < 360°$.

Solution:

Move everything to one side:

$\sin \theta \cos \theta - \sin \theta = 0$

Factor out $\sin \theta$:

$\sin \theta(\cos \theta - 1) = 0$

So either $\sin \theta = 0$ or $\cos \theta - 1 = 0$:

  • $\sin \theta = 0$: $\theta = 0°, 180°$
  • $\cos \theta = 1$: $\theta = 0°$

Therefore: $\theta = 0°, 180°$ (note that 0° satisfies both conditions)

Common Mistake: Never divide both sides of an equation by a trigonometric expression like $\sin \theta$ or $\cos \theta$—you might lose solutions! Always factor instead.

In Example 7, if we had divided by $\sin \theta$, we would have lost the solutions where $\sin \theta = 0$.

Practice Problem 10: Solve $2\cos^2 \theta - \cos \theta = 0$ for $0° \leq \theta < 360°$.
Solution:
Factor: $\cos \theta(2\cos \theta - 1) = 0$
So $\cos \theta = 0$ or $\cos \theta = 1/2$
$\cos \theta = 0$: $\theta = 90°, 270°$
$\cos \theta = 1/2$: $\theta = 60°, 300°$
Therefore: $\theta = 60°, 90°, 270°, 300°$
Practice Problem 11: Solve $\tan^2 x - 3 = 0$ for $0 \leq x < 2\pi$.
Solution:
$\tan^2 x = 3$, so $\tan x = \pm\sqrt{3}$
$\tan x = \sqrt{3}$: $x = \pi/3, 4\pi/3$
$\tan x = -\sqrt{3}$: $x = 2\pi/3, 5\pi/3$
Therefore: $x = \frac{\pi}{3}, \frac{2\pi}{3}, \frac{4\pi}{3}, \frac{5\pi}{3}$
Practice Problem 12: Solve $\sin(2\theta) + \cos \theta = 0$ for $0° \leq \theta < 360°$.
Solution:
Use $\sin(2\theta) = 2\sin \theta \cos \theta$:
$2\sin \theta \cos \theta + \cos \theta = 0$
Factor: $\cos \theta(2\sin \theta + 1) = 0$
$\cos \theta = 0$: $\theta = 90°, 270°$
$\sin \theta = -1/2$: $\theta = 210°, 330°$
Therefore: $\theta = 90°, 210°, 270°, 330°$

Summary: Your Trigonometric Identity Toolkit

We've covered a comprehensive set of trigonometric identities. Here's a summary of the most important ones:

Pythagorean Identities
$\sin^2 \theta + \cos^2 \theta = 1$
$1 + \tan^2 \theta = \sec^2 \theta$
$1 + \cot^2 \theta = \csc^2 \theta$
Even-Odd Identities
$\sin(-\theta) = -\sin \theta$
$\cos(-\theta) = \cos \theta$
$\tan(-\theta) = -\tan \theta$
Sum and Difference Formulas
$\sin(A \pm B) = \sin A \cos B \pm \cos A \sin B$ $\cos(A \pm B) = \cos A \cos B \mp \sin A \sin B$
Double Angle Formulas
$\sin(2\theta) = 2\sin \theta \cos \theta$
$\cos(2\theta) = \cos^2 \theta - \sin^2 \theta = 2\cos^2 \theta - 1 = 1 - 2\sin^2 \theta$

Key Takeaways

  • Identities are equations that are true for all values (where defined), unlike equations which are true only for specific values
  • The Pythagorean identity $\sin^2 \theta + \cos^2 \theta = 1$ is fundamental and follows directly from the unit circle definition
  • Sum and difference formulas allow us to find exact values for angles like 15°, 75°, etc.
  • Double and half angle formulas are special cases derived from sum formulas
  • Product-to-sum formulas enabled practical computation before calculators and remain important in signal processing
  • When solving trigonometric equations, use identities to simplify to a single function when possible, and always factor rather than divide

Practice Problem 13: Prove the identity: $\frac{1 - \cos(2\theta)}{\sin(2\theta)} = \tan \theta$
Solution:
Start with the left side and use double angle formulas:
$\frac{1 - \cos(2\theta)}{\sin(2\theta)} = \frac{1 - (1 - 2\sin^2 \theta)}{2\sin \theta \cos \theta}$
$= \frac{2\sin^2 \theta}{2\sin \theta \cos \theta} = \frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta} = \tan \theta$
Practice Problem 14: Simplify: $\cos^4 x - \sin^4 x$
Solution:
Factor as a difference of squares:
$\cos^4 x - \sin^4 x = (\cos^2 x + \sin^2 x)(\cos^2 x - \sin^2 x)$
$= (1)(\cos^2 x - \sin^2 x) = \cos(2x)$
(using the Pythagorean identity and double angle formula)
Practice Problem 15: Solve $\cos^2 x - \sin^2 x = \sin x$ for $0 \leq x < 2\pi$.
Solution:
Use the double angle formula $\cos^2 x - \sin^2 x = \cos(2x)$:
$\cos(2x) = \sin x$
Use $\cos(2x) = 1 - 2\sin^2 x$:
$1 - 2\sin^2 x = \sin x$
$2\sin^2 x + \sin x - 1 = 0$
$(2\sin x - 1)(\sin x + 1) = 0$
$\sin x = 1/2$ or $\sin x = -1$
$\sin x = 1/2$: $x = \pi/6, 5\pi/6$
$\sin x = -1$: $x = 3\pi/2$
Therefore: $x = \frac{\pi}{6}, \frac{5\pi}{6}, \frac{3\pi}{2}$
Practice Problem 16: If $\sin \theta = 4/5$ and $90° < \theta < 180°$, find the exact value of $\sin(2\theta)$, $\cos(2\theta)$, and $\tan(2\theta)$.
Solution:
First find $\cos \theta$: $\cos^2 \theta = 1 - (4/5)^2 = 1 - 16/25 = 9/25$
Since $\theta$ is in Quadrant II: $\cos \theta = -3/5$

$\sin(2\theta) = 2\sin \theta \cos \theta = 2(4/5)(-3/5) = -24/25$

$\cos(2\theta) = \cos^2 \theta - \sin^2 \theta = 9/25 - 16/25 = -7/25$

$\tan(2\theta) = \frac{\sin(2\theta)}{\cos(2\theta)} = \frac{-24/25}{-7/25} = \frac{24}{7}$
